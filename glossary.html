Glossary

Short definitions of historical, religious, and political terms used throughout this archive — for readers who may be encountering some of this terminology for the first time. Several of these terms are used differently by different groups; where that's the case, this glossary notes it rather than picking a side.

Communalism

In the South Asian context, the organisation of politics, identity, or conflict along religious community lines (for example, Hindu–Muslim or Hindu–Sikh) — often associated with sectarian tension or violence between groups defined by religion.

Cow Slaughter

The killing of cows or cattle for meat or other purposes. Cows are widely considered sacred in Hinduism, and cow slaughter has long been a flashpoint for religious and political tension in South Asia; several Indian states restrict or ban it.

Cultural Genocide

The deliberate destruction of a group's cultural heritage — language, religious sites, traditions, and practices — as a means of erasing its identity, distinct from (though sometimes alongside) physical violence against the group's members. It is not part of the legal definition of genocide under the 1948 UN Genocide Convention, though the concept is discussed in genocide studies and cultural heritage law.

Demographic Change

Shifts over time in the religious or ethnic composition of a population in a given region. In South Asia, demographic change is both studied empirically by demographers (using census data) and invoked in political debate around migration, conversion, and birth rates.

Dhimmi

A historical legal status under Islamic rule for non-Muslim subjects — including Hindus, Christians, and Jews in various empires — who were permitted to practise their religion in exchange for paying a special tax (see Jizya) and accepting certain restrictions.

Ethnic Cleansing

The forced removal of an ethnic or religious group from a particular area through violence, intimidation, or coercion, with the aim of making that area homogeneous. The term is widely used in international law and journalism, though — unlike genocide — it is not itself a separately defined crime under international law.

Forced Conversion

Compelling a person to adopt a different religion against their will, through violence, coercion, deception, or severe social or economic pressure — as distinct from voluntary religious conversion.

Genocide

Under the 1948 UN Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide, acts committed with intent to destroy, in whole or in part, a national, ethnic, racial, or religious group — including killing and causing serious bodily or mental harm to members of the group. Whether a specific historical episode meets this legal definition is often debated by historians and legal scholars, separately from whether it was a serious atrocity.

Ghazi

An honorific title in Islamic history for a victorious military leader, particularly one who fought against non-Muslim populations or states. Several historical figures referenced in this archive's timeline were given or adopted this title.

Iconoclasm

The deliberate destruction of religious images, idols, or symbols, typically for religious or ideological reasons. In the South Asian context, this most often refers to the destruction or desecration of Hindu temples and deities during periods of conquest.

Islamic Terrorism

A term for acts of terrorism carried out by individuals or groups who cite Islamic texts or teachings as justification. It is widely used in media, government, and academic contexts, but is also debated: some object to linking a religion broadly with the actions of specific extremist groups, while others argue that the religious framing used by such groups is itself significant and shouldn't be omitted.

Jihad

An Arabic term meaning "struggle" or "effort." In Islamic theology it covers a broad range of meanings, including an inner spiritual struggle (sometimes called the "greater jihad") and, in some contexts, armed struggle (the "lesser jihad"). The term is also used — including in this archive — to refer to specific historical military campaigns and conflicts.

Jizya

A per-capita tax historically levied on non-Muslim subjects (dhimmis) living under Islamic rule, in exchange for protection and exemption from obligations — such as military service — that applied to Muslim subjects.

Kafir

An Arabic term meaning "disbeliever" or "infidel," referring to a person who does not believe in Islam. It appears in Islamic legal and theological texts to describe non-Muslims.

Love Jihad

A term, used primarily in India, for an alleged organised effort by Muslim men to convert Hindu (and sometimes Christian) women to Islam through marriage or romantic relationships. The term and the claims behind it are used by some Hindu organisations and have been the basis for legislation in several Indian states; the existence of any organised campaign, as opposed to individual interfaith relationships, is disputed by other researchers, journalists, and civil liberties groups.

Mob Lynching

The killing of a person by a group acting outside any legal process — typically based on accusation or suspicion (for example, of cow slaughter, blasphemy, or theft) — without trial.

Naxalite

A member or supporter of Maoist/Communist insurgent groups active in parts of India since the late 1960s, named after the Naxalbari uprising. Naxalite groups have been involved in armed conflict with the Indian state and, in some documented cases, violence against Hindu religious figures and communities in affected regions.

Partition of India

The 1947 division of British India into the independent states of India and Pakistan (Pakistan initially comprised West and East wings, the latter becoming Bangladesh in 1971). It was accompanied by large-scale communal violence and one of the largest mass migrations in history — estimates of deaths range from several hundred thousand to around two million, with an estimated 10–20 million people displaced.

Pogrom

An organised attack — often officially sanctioned or tolerated — on a particular ethnic or religious group, typically involving violence against people and destruction of their property.

Razakar

A paramilitary militia that operated in Hyderabad State in 1947–48, associated with violence against Hindus during the period surrounding the Indian government's annexation of Hyderabad ("Operation Polo").

Shariah

Islamic religious law, derived from the Quran and Hadith, covering religious obligations as well as — in various historical and contemporary contexts — aspects of civil and criminal law.

Slavery

The practice of treating people as property, deprived of personal freedom and forced to work or serve. In South Asian history, the enslavement of non-Muslim populations — including Hindus — was practised by various Islamic states and empires, particularly following military conquests.
