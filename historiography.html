Historiography & Methodology

A guide to how this archive is built: the sources it draws on, the limitations of those sources, and the principles that govern what is documented here and how.

Introduction

The history of Hindu persecution is, in large part, a history of what was not recorded — or what was recorded only by those who carried it out. For much of the period covered by this archive, the primary witnesses to violence against Hindu communities were either the perpetrators themselves, court scribes in their employ, or foreign observers whose accounts survive only in fragments. The communities most affected — village populations, temple congregations, displaced families — left few written records of their own. Their history survives, where it survives at all, in oral tradition, in temple inscriptions, in the physical evidence of destroyed sites, and occasionally in the accounts of sympathetic outsiders.

This page explains what sources this archive draws on, what their limitations are, and the principles that govern how their evidence is used. Every figure cited, every incident documented, and every classification applied in this archive reflects choices — about what counts as sufficient evidence, about how to handle conflicting accounts, about where to draw the boundary between what is documented and what is merely plausible. Those choices are explained here.

Primary source categories

Court chronicles and sultanate records

The most extensive written record of the medieval period comes from court chronicles — histories commissioned by and written for the rulers who carried out the events they describe. Works such as al-Utbi's Kitab-i-Yamini (on Mahmud of Ghazni), Ziauddin Barani's Tarikh-i-Firuz Shahi, and Timur's own Tuzk-i-Timuri are primary sources in the strict sense: they were written by contemporaries, often eyewitnesses or participants. They are also, without exception, written from the perspective of the victors, with explicit religious framing. Figures given in these chronicles for casualties, enslaved populations, and temple destructions should be read as the authors' own claims — neither uncritically accepted nor reflexively discounted. Where multiple chronicles give comparable figures independently, that convergence carries more weight than any single account. Where figures appear only in a single source with obvious rhetorical purpose (recording an impressive victory), this archive notes the limitation.

Later Islamic historians

A secondary layer of historical writing — produced in the Mughal period and after — synthesises earlier chronicles into broader historical narratives. Ferishta's Tarikh-i-Ferishta (17th century) is the most widely cited example: a comprehensive history of Muslim rule in India that draws on earlier sultanate records. These sources are one step removed from the events they describe, but they often preserve material from chronicles that no longer survive in their original form. The same caveats apply as to the primary chronicles, with the additional note that later historians sometimes inflated figures for rhetorical or literary effect.

Colonial-era historians and administrators

British colonial records — census data, administrative reports, judicial proceedings, and the work of historians in the colonial tradition — provide a different kind of documentation, particularly useful for the 18th, 19th, and early 20th centuries. Colonial historians brought their own biases: some minimised Hindu-Muslim conflict for administrative reasons; others over-dramatised it. But the raw administrative data — population figures, land records, accounts of specific incidents — is often the most systematic evidence available for this period. Will Durant's synthesis in The Story of Civilisation, though not itself a primary source, drew on this material alongside the chronicle tradition and remains a reference point for the scale of medieval violence.

20th-century journalistic and human rights documentation

For incidents from the 20th century onward, this archive draws on contemporary journalism, human rights reports (Amnesty International, Human Rights Watch, minority rights organisations), and academic scholarship. These sources are subject to their own limitations — journalistic accounts reflect editorial choices about which communities' suffering is considered newsworthy, and human rights organisations have not always applied consistent standards across religious communities. Where possible, multiple independent sources are cited for the same incident, and discrepancies between accounts are noted rather than smoothed over.

Survivor testimony and oral history

For some incidents — particularly the Noakhali genocide, the exodus of Kashmiri Hindus, and the persecution of Hindu minorities in Bangladesh — survivor testimony is among the most important available evidence. These accounts are cited where they have been recorded and published (by journalists, human rights organisations, or researchers), with attribution to the original source. Oral accounts that have not been independently recorded or verified are not cited as evidence for specific figures, though they inform the broader historical understanding. This archive treats survivor testimony with the seriousness it deserves: lived experience is not a lesser form of evidence than a court chronicle, and the communities most likely to dismiss it are often those with the least at stake in the accuracy of the record.
